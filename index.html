Most rare diseases have no approved treatment.

The opportunity

Bridging complex system mechanics with predictive AI, I am building data frameworks to search the medicines we already have for treatments that rare-disease patients have never been offered. Somewhere within the thousands of approved, well-understood drugs are likely treatments for diseases they were never designed for—hidden in the dense web of known links between drugs, genes, proteins, and clinical conditions.

This work builds and rigorously validates an AI system that reads that web and predicts which existing drugs are the strongest candidates for passed-over diseases. Built on TxGNN, a leading method for the task, this pipeline pushes the architecture where it is weakest: rare diseases, where there is barely any data to learn from.

How a prediction becomes a candidate

A prediction is only worth anything if it is trustworthy, plausible, and safe.

Each stage below is a gate the candidate has to pass before it earns any further attention.

01

Temporal validation

An AI model can accidentally learn from information that did not exist yet, which makes it look far cleverer than it is. Here the model is rebuilt so it only ever learns from the past, then tested on what came afterwards — so a prediction is a genuine forecast rather than hindsight dressed up as insight.

02

The rare-disease engine

Rare diseases are hard for AI precisely because so little is known about them. Three complementary methods each draw on a different kind of evidence that is available, and together they give a sparse disease far more to stand on.

Phenotype-guided augmentation

Links a poorly-understood rare disease to better-studied ones by comparing their symptom patterns — what the disease actually looks like in patients.

Genetic anchor propagation

For diseases traced to a single faulty gene, predictions are anchored to that genetic root, carrying signal directly from the cause.

Zero-shot ontology transfer

Places a disease within the medical family tree and borrows knowledge from its closest relatives.

03

Molecular docking

The strongest predictions are taken into a 3D simulation that tests whether the drug molecule actually fits and binds to its biological target.

04

Toxicity screening

Every surviving candidate is screened for known contraindications and likely adverse effects, so safety is weighed before anything is put forward.

05

Novelty check

What remains is cross-referenced against existing clinical trials, published literature, and patents — separating what is genuinely new from what is already being pursued.

On the horizon

Understanding why, not only what.

The pipeline today tells you which drugs are linked to improvement. The next phase works toward understanding why they work — and whether they would still work if we actually intervened.
